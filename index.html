When it was established in 1753 The British Museum became the first public national museum in the world. Over 250 hundred years later it has emmassed a collection of nearly eight million works making it one of the largest in the world.

Emily Duthie remarks in her article for Public History Review, "museum building in Britain in the nineteenth century was a direct consequence of war, colonialism and missionary expeditions". The British Museum is the embodiment of this colonial legacy, with the size of its collection oweing much to the expanse of the British Empire. Knowing this makes it hard for the visitor shake the feeling that the items being observed have been misappropriated. That their native homes may never see them again, even if they make international pleas for their return as is the case with the Rosetta Stone and Elgin Marbles.

Not all of the collection was acquired in this way, however. Items were donated for the purpose of preservation, loaned from other institutions or purchased using the British Museum's considerable budget. These items are all on display too, however identifying them is not an easy task.

Given the scale of the collection it is evident that a large team was employed to digitise it. It was widely reported that Google was working alongside the British Museum to help people explore their collection. Whether they worked on the core website is unclear, however they were directly involved in the creation of the sister website The Museum of the World' and also the Google Street View interactive tour. Evidently the British Museum has had significant technological backing to bring its collection online.
